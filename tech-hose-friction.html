Fluid Dynamics: Reducing Friction in Long Hoses

Head Pressure Loss

When you cannot drive the truck to the slope, you have to drag the hose. Pumping a thick mulch slurry through 300 to 500 feet of hose creates massive back-pressure. Every foot of hose creates friction, resisting the flow. If the friction becomes too great, the pump dead-heads, or the slurry slows to a trickle at the nozzle, making it impossible to reach the top of the cut.

Operators often try to solve this by thinning the mix with water, but this dilutes the tackifier and ruins the application specs.

Shear-Thinning Chemistry

OHRA-BOND™ GREEN is a "Shear-Thinning" fluid. This means its viscosity is inversely proportional to the shear stress applied. When the fluid is moving fast inside the hose (high shear), its polymer chains align in the direction of flow, drastically reducing its apparent viscosity and friction coefficient.

This allows you to pump a thick, high-solids slurry through 500 feet of hose as if it were water. But the moment it exits the nozzle and hits the soil (zero shear), the polymer chains relax and tangle, instantly regaining their high viscosity (3250 cps) to stick to the slope. This "Jekyll and Hyde" behavior is critical for remote access applications.
