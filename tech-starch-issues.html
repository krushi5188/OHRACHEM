Rainfastness: Why Starch Binders Fail in Tropics

The Solubility Trap

Corn starch and potato starch are cheap. This makes them attractive ingredients for budget-brand tackifiers. However, unmodified starch has a fatal flaw: it is highly water-soluble. While it creates a hard, brittle crust when dry, as soon as it is re-wetted by rain, it dissolves back into a liquid and washes away.

This is the "Solubility Trap." The binder works perfectly on a sunny day, but fails catastrophically during the first heavy storm—exactly when you need erosion control the most. In tropical climates or monsoon seasons, starch-based binders are effectively useless.

Hydrocolloid Tackiness

OHRA-BOND™ GREEN works on a different principle. It is a hydrocolloid, meaning it loves water ("hydro-philic"). When it rains, instead of dissolving, the gum absorbs the water and swells. This swelling action actually makes the binder stickier (more tacky). It turns the rain into a glue that locks the mulch and soil even tighter.

We call this property "Rainfastness." While no organic binder is permanent, a high-quality hydrocolloid will endure multiple heavy rain cycles without washing out. It remains active and effective until the vegetation is established, providing reliable protection in the harshest weather.
