Pump Wear Analysis: Lubricity of Hydrocolloids

The Hidden Cost of Cheap Binders

Many low-cost tackifiers achieve their "bulk" by including inert fillers like silica sand, clay, or gypsum. While these fillers make the bag feel heavy and lower the price per pound, they act like sandpaper inside your hydroseeder. The tight tolerances of a gear pump or centrifugal pump volute are rapidly eroded by these abrasive particulates, leading to premature seal failure and loss of pressure.

Replacing a main pump seal or an entire pump assembly can cost thousands of dollars and result in days of downtime. When you calculate the "Total Cost of Ownership," cheap abrasive binders are significantly more expensive than pure chemistry.

Vegetable Mucilage as a Coolant

OHRA-BOND™ GREEN is 95% pure vegetable hydrocolloid. It contains zero crystalline silica or mineral grit. In fact, the mucilage created when it hydrates acts as a high-performance lubricant. It coats the internal metal surfaces of the pump, reducing friction and heat generation.

Mechanics often report that pumps running our hydrocolloid remain cooler during operation compared to those pumping water or clay-based slurries. By switching to a non-abrasive binder, contractors can extend the maintenance intervals of their equipment and protect their capital investment.
