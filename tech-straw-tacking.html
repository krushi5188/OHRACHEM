Crimping vs. Chemical Tacking: Cost Review

The Old School Way

Mechanical crimping involves driving a tractor with a specialized disc implement over spread straw to punch it into the soil. While effective on flat ground, it has significant limitations. It is slow, requires a dedicated tractor and operator, and is dangerous or impossible on steep slopes. Furthermore, crimping physically disturbs the soil surface, potentially increasing erosion risk if the straw blows out.

The Hydro-Tacking Advantage

Chemical tacking (Hydro-Tacking) involves spraying a slurry of water, binder, and a small amount of wood fiber (trace mulch) over the spread straw. This "glues" the straw fibers to each other and to the soil surface without mechanical disturbance.

Cost Benefit: A hydroseeder can tack a slope from the roadside 30% faster than a tractor can crimp it. It eliminates the need for a second machine (the tractor) and creates a continuous chemical net that resists high winds far better than crimped straw. On 2:1 slopes where tractors cannot go, hydro-tacking is the only viable option.
