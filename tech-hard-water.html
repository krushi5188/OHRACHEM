Water Quality: Calcium Hardness Effects on Swell

Cationic Interference

Not all water is created equal. Hydroseeding contractors often pull water from whatever source is closest to the job site: municipal hydrants, ponds, creeks, or agricultural wells. "Hard water" contains high levels of dissolved minerals, specifically divalent cations like Calcium (Ca++) and Magnesium (Mg++).

These positively charged ions act as "cross-linkers" in the wrong way. They bind tightly to the anionic sites on the polymer chain, preventing it from unfolding fully. This is called "cationic interference." In extremely hard water (>300 ppm hardness), the swell volume of a hydrocolloid can be reduced by up to 20%.

Know Your Source

If you notice your slurry looks thinner than usual despite using the same mixing ratio, check your water source. Hard water suppresses viscosity yield.
The Fix: You don't need expensive water softeners. Simply compensate by increasing the application rate. If you are pumping hard well water, add 10% more OHRA-BOND™ per tank to achieve the same target viscosity on the slope. Field awareness of water chemistry prevents under-application.
