The Swell Factor: Why 42ml/g is the Benchmark

Water Holding Capacity

Erosion control is not just about gluing soil; it is about establishing vegetation. Seeds need constant moisture to germinate. In arid climates, the ability of the hydroseeding matrix to retain water is the difference between a lush green slope and a barren brown hill.

Many cheap tackifiers add weight and viscosity but do not absorb significant water. They act as "fillers." We measure performance by "Swell Volume"—the amount of water (in milliliters) that 1 gram of binder can absorb and hold in a gel state.

The Data: >35ml/g

Standard guar gum typically swells to 20-25 ml/g. Starch binders are even lower, often dissolving completely rather than swelling. OHRA-BOND™ GREEN consistently tests at 42 ml/g. This means for every pound of binder you apply, you are creating a localized moisture reservoir of over 4 gallons of water directly around the seed.

The "Moisture Battery"

Think of the hydrocolloid as a "moisture battery." During a rain event or irrigation, it swells rapidly, capturing water that would otherwise run off. As the soil dries, the hydrocolloid slowly releases this stored moisture back to the seed. This buffers the seedling against drought stress and extends the window of viability between rain events. High swell volume is the single most important chemical property for germination success.
